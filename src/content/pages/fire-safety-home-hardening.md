Home hardening means upgrading the materials and design of your house to resist ember intrusion and radiant heat. It's the single most effective thing you can do to protect your property.

## Zone Zero (0–5 feet from your home)

California now requires ember-resistant landscaping within 5 feet of all structures in fire hazard zones. This is the most critical area.

**Required actions:**
- Remove all combustible plants, mulch, and debris
- Replace wood fencing attachments with metal
- Clear leaves from under decks and porches
- Move firewood and propane tanks at least 30 feet away

## Roofing

**Requirement:** Class A fire-rated materials.

Options include composition shingles, concrete tile, metal roofing, or clay tile. Wood shakes are NOT compliant. Clean gutters regularly — accumulated debris is fuel.

## Windows

**Recommendation:** Double-pane or tempered glass.

Single-pane windows can shatter from radiant heat, allowing embers inside. Dual-pane glass is significantly more resistant.

## Vents

**Requirement:** 1/8" steel mesh screening on all exterior vents (attic, soffit, crawlspace, dryer).

Aluminum mesh is NOT sufficient — it melts at wildfire temperatures. Steel mesh blocks ember entry, which is the #1 cause of home ignition during wildfires.

## Siding and Exterior Walls

Stucco, fiber cement, and brick are the most fire-resistant options. Wood siding can be treated but is inherently more vulnerable.

## Decks and Fencing

- Clear all combustible material from under decks
- Use fire-resistant decking materials where possible
- Detach wood fencing from the house with a metal post or non-combustible section
